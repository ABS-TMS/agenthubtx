Groundwater District Questions to Ask

When ¶ 7(I) is checked on a TREC 20-19 contract — what to ask before the option period expires

This guide is a practice aid for agents and does not constitute legal advice. For transactions with significant groundwater concerns, consult a licensed Texas water attorney. Questions reference TREC No. 20-19, paragraph 7(I), effective July 1, 2026.
The disclosure tells you a groundwater district exists. It tells you nothing about whether that district is actively restricting production. These questions fill that gap.
The Disclosure Just Says a District Exists — These Tell You What Kind
About the District
Which district?

Which groundwater conservation district governs this property — and is there more than one with jurisdiction?

Active or administrative?

Is the district actively managing and restricting production, or essentially administrative? Not all districts are equally aggressive.

Current restrictions?

Is the district currently under any pumping restrictions, moratoriums, or allocation caps?

Future conditions?

Has the district issued any desired future conditions or managed available groundwater findings that could limit future production?

If the Property Has a Well, Confirm It Actually Works
The Well Itself
Last tested?

When was the well last tested for both water quality and production rate?

TCEQ report on file?

Is there a current water well report on file with the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality?

Production history?

Has the well ever gone dry, been re-drilled, or had significant production decline?

Nearby Users Can Draw Down the Same Aquifer
What's Happening Around the Property
High-volume neighbors?

Are there any high-volume users nearby — agriculture, municipalities, or industrial operations — drawing from the same aquifer?

New permits nearby?

Has the district received any new large-volume permit applications in this area recently?

What This Property Is Actually Entitled to Draw
The Property's Specific Water Rights
Permitted well?

Does the property have a permitted well — and if so, what is the permitted annual volume?

Permit transferable?

Is that permit transferable to a new owner, or does it require re-application after closing?

Current usage?

Has the seller been drawing against that permit, and how much of the annual allocation has been used in recent years?

Pending disputes?

Are there any pending permit renewals, challenges, or disputes with the district on this property?

What to Do With the Answers You Get
Direction for Agents
Ask before option expires

These questions need answers inside the option period — not after closing. If the listing agent can't or won't answer, that itself is information.

The disclosure isn't enough

Paragraph 7(I) being checked tells the buyer a district exists. It says nothing about whether that district is about to restrict the property's water supply.

Flag the specialist option

Worth telling the buyer directly: this is one of the situations where getting a water attorney or hydrologist involved during the option period is genuinely cheap compared to the alternative.

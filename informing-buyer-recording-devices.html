Informing Your Buyer About Recording Devices

The MLS has a field for this. It gets skimmed past constantly — and that's exactly when a buyer's candid conversation ends up on someone else's camera.

68% of people believe smart home devices "listen" to them even when they're not aware of it — this isn't a paranoid concern, it's the mainstream expectation. The MLS surveillance disclosure exists because of exactly that reality.
The gap this closes: most MLS listings include a field disclosing that video and/or audio surveillance is in use at the property. Agents check it, register that it exists, and move on to scheduling the showing — without ever telling their own buyer client. The disclosure did its job. The buyer just never heard about it.
It's Not Just Ring and Nest
Devices That May Be Watching or Listening

Smart phones · webcams / home security cameras · baby monitors · voice-activated USB drives · wireless Bluetooth speakers · voice-recording pens · smart TVs (Amazon Fire TV) · Xbox Kinect · Amazon Echo / Google Home / Alexa / Siri / Cortana · Nest Cam · Ring / Skybell doorbells · smart outdoor security lighting with camera · Petcube-style wifi pet cameras

Assume any always-on, internet-connected device could be listening

Almost anything that's part of the "Internet of Things" and connects to an app has this capability, whether or not it's marketed as a security device.

Before You Even Schedule the Showing
Check the MLS Field
Look for it every time, not just when it seems obvious

A home with an alarm sign in the yard isn't the only one recording — plenty of ordinary-looking listings have Ring, Nest, or similar devices disclosed in that field.

Don't rely on memory across multiple showings in one day

If you're touring several homes back to back, check this fresh for each one — it's easy to assume "probably none of them do" after the first two don't.

Before Your Buyer Walks In, Every Time
Brief Your Client First
This takes ten seconds and prevents a real problem

Tell your buyer before you're both standing in the entryway, not after they've already said something they'd rather have kept private.

Sample Script — Say This Before Walking In

"Quick heads up before we go in — this listing has a camera with audio recording. Let's save any real thoughts about the house for the car afterward, just to be safe."

Protect Your Client's Strategy
What Not to Discuss Inside
Offer price thinking

"I think we could get this for $20K under ask" is exactly the kind of thing you don't want the listing side hearing back.

How motivated your buyer actually is

Any comment revealing urgency, a moving deadline, or "this is our top choice" undercuts your client's negotiating position if it's ever heard.

Perceived flaws you plan to use in negotiation

Pointing out repair issues out loud inside the home telegraphs your inspection-and-negotiate strategy before you've even made an offer.

Over-enthusiasm about specific features

Gushing over the kitchen or the backyard tips your hand just as much as words do — assume body language and reactions are being watched even where audio isn't a concern.

Neighbors are often nosier than the owner

If you're on the phone or talking outside, remember a home's own recording devices aren't the only listening risk — a neighbor within earshot is a real, easy-to-forget possibility.

A practical trick if you're genuinely worried

Running a faucet creates white noise that makes it harder for a nearby microphone to pick up clear conversation — useful in a pinch, not a substitute for just stepping outside to talk.

Why This Is a Fiduciary Duty Issue, Not Just Courtesy
The Bigger Picture
You represent your buyer's interests — including their privacy

Letting your client unknowingly reveal strategy or motivation to the seller's side is a real disadvantage you could have prevented with one sentence before walking in.

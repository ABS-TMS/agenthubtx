The Three License Tiers

Only one of these three can inspect independently, without sponsorship.

Apprentice Inspector Entry-level. Must train under the direct supervision of a Professional Real Estate Inspector — cannot inspect independently.
Real Estate Inspector Intermediate. Requires experience and education beyond the Apprentice tier, plus passing an exam — but still requires indirect supervision by a Professional Real Estate Inspector.
Professional Real Estate Inspector The only tier that can perform inspections without sponsorship by another license holder. Also the tier that may supervise Apprentice and Real Estate Inspectors.

What to Check Before Recommending an Inspector

  • Confirm the license is active and in good standing on TREC's search tool — not just that a license exists
  • Confirm which tier they hold — an Apprentice or Real Estate Inspector should be operating under a Professional Real Estate Inspector's supervision
  • Check for any disciplinary history listed on their public license record

A Conflict of Interest Rule Worth Knowing

An inspector can't act in a dual capacity. Under Texas Occupations Code §1102.303, a person can't act as both inspector and broker/sales agent in the same transaction, and can't inspect on behalf of an undisclosed principalOcc. Code §1102.303. If you're also a licensed inspector, this means you cannot inspect a property your own brokerage has listed, or one where you were personally involved as the agent.

Separately, TREC Rule 535.156 requires a license holder to disclose to their client any relationship they have with an inspector they recommendRule 535.156 — worth keeping in mind if you regularly refer the same inspector to clients.

01

Broker Supervision & Sponsorship

Does my broker have to personally supervise everything I do day to day?
No. A broker can delegate supervisory responsibility to someone else with the right experience and expertise. That said, the broker still remains responsible for their agents' authorized acts, and a complaint against a sponsored agent is also treated as a complaint against the broker for purposes of evaluating whether proper supervision was in place.
Source: TREC Rule 535.2(e); TRELA §1101.803; Rule 535.141(c)–(d)
Can I work out of a different office location than my sponsoring broker's main office?
Yes — but you must still be associated with, and acting for, your actual sponsoring broker at all times. You can't do brokerage work for a different broker or out of a different broker's office while sponsored elsewhere.
Source: TRELA §1101.803; Rule 535.2(a); TRELA §1101.351(c)
If I switch sponsoring brokers, do I get to take my listings or buyer clients with me?
No. Listing agreements and buyer representation agreements are private contracts between the client and the broker — not the individual agent. The client stays with your previous broker unless they're released from that agreement.
Source: TREC FAQ, brokerage relationships
Can I receive a commission through my old broker after I've moved my sponsorship?
Yes. You can be paid through your current sponsoring broker, or through the broker who sponsored you at the time you actually earned the right to that compensation.
Source: TREC Rule 535.3
02

Forms & Contracts

Does TREC have a promulgated listing agreement or buyer representation agreement form?
No to both. These are private contracts between the broker and the client, not regulated or supplied by TREC. ABS's default is TXR 1101 paragraph 5A as the standard listing agreement — check with your broker before deviating from that.
Source: TREC FAQ, forms; ABS internal policy
Am I required to use TREC's adopted contract forms?
Yes, with limited exceptions outlined in Rule 537.11(a). TREC can and does discipline agents who fail to use an adopted form when required. You're also prohibited from adding anything beyond factual matters or business details to a TREC-mandated form.
Source: TRELA §1101.155; Rule 537.11
How are days counted in a TREC contract?
Counting starts the day after the contract's effective date, and every day counts as a calendar day — not a business day, unless the specific provision says otherwise.
Source: TREC FAQ, contracts
Can a non-license holder use TREC's promulgated forms?
Yes, they're available for public use — but they're built for use by trained, licensed agents. Anyone using one without that training assumes the risk of a mistake that could create financial loss or an unenforceable contract.
Source: TREC FAQ, forms
03

Advertising

If my name or team name appears on a sign, does the broker's name have to be there too?
Yes — and the broker's name must appear at least half the size of the largest contact information on the sign or ad. This applies to building signs, promotional items, and sponsorships, not just yard signs.
Source: TREC Rule 535.155(b)(1) and (b)(3)
Can I advertise under a name like "Sarah's Spectacular Properties" instead of my own licensed name?
Not without registering it properly. An individual sales agent generally can't use an assumed business name — anything other than your licensed name or a registered alternate name counts as a "team name" and has to meet TREC's team name requirements, including the broker's name appearing alongside it.
Source: TRELA §1101.652(b)(23); Rule 535.154(a)(5)
Does TREC review my advertising before I post it?
No. TREC only discusses advertising compliance with the broker directly — your sponsoring broker is responsible for reviewing your advertising and maintaining written policies to ensure it complies with TREC's rules. Both you and your broker can be disciplined if it doesn't.
Source: TREC Rules 535.2(g), (i)(6), 535.154, 535.155; TRELA §1101.652(b)(23)
Does putting my email or website URL in an ad count as advertising on its own?
Not by itself. But if that URL or email implies you're personally responsible for a brokerage (when you're a sales agent, not the broker), or if it otherwise makes the ad misleading, it becomes a violation.
Source: TREC Rule 535.155(d)(4) and (b)(4); TRELA §1101.652(b)(23)
04

Agency Disclosure & IABS

Do I have to give the IABS form to everyone I meet, including at an open house?
No. The IABS disclosure isn't required at an open house (for that same property), for a residential lease under one year where a sale isn't being considered, or when meeting someone you already know is represented by another agent.
Source: TRELA §1101.558(c)
Is putting a link to the IABS form in my email signature enough to satisfy the disclosure requirement?
No. A link in a footer or signature block alone doesn't count. The IABS form has to be delivered in person, by mail, in the body of an email, or as an attachment/link with a specific reference to it in the body of the email.
Source: TREC Rule 531.20(d)
If I'm representing the buyer, does the listing agent have to tell me they represent the seller?
Yes — on the first contact between you, the listing agent has to disclose that they represent the seller, the same way you're required to disclose your own representation when setting up a showing.
Source: TRELA §1101.558(b)
If I'm buying, selling, or leasing property for a family member, do I have to disclose that I'm licensed?
Yes, and it has to be in writing. You can't use your license expertise to the disadvantage of the person you're dealing with, even if it's a relative.
Source: TREC Rule 535.144(c)
05

Inspections & Disclosure

If an inspector misses something, does the buyer have any recourse against the inspector?
Usually not. Inspectors follow the Standards of Practice, which only require a visual inspection — they're not required to dig up buried lines, predict the lifespan of mechanical components, or catch every possible future failure.
Source: TREC Rule 535.227(b)(3)(C)(i); TREC FAQ, inspections
Can a license holder be disciplined for knowingly failing to disclose a defect to a buyer?
Yes. A license holder who makes a knowing misrepresentation or fails to disclose a known latent defect can face Commission discipline — this applies even if it's the seller, not the agent, who withheld the information from the agent in the first place, once the agent independently knows about it.
Source: TRELA §1101.652(b)(3)
Can I act as both the inspector and the agent on the same transaction?
No. An inspector cannot act in the dual capacity of inspector and broker/sales agent (or an undisclosed principal) on the same deal — this is a violation regardless of how the relationship is structured.
Source: Texas Occupations Code §1102.303
Does TREC require inspectors to use a pre-inspection contract?
No. Inspectors aren't required to sign a contract before performing an inspection. Some choose to use one anyway, often drafted by a private attorney or provided by an inspector trade association.
Source: TREC FAQ, inspections

Open Houses, Intermediary & Notices

← Back to Tools Directory
Not legal advice. This reflects TREC's current published guidance and Texas Occupations Code as of 2026, including the January 1, 2026 written buyer-agreement changes. Rules change, and your specific situation may involve nuance this page can't capture — when in doubt, ask your designated supervisor or your broker before acting.

The Two Rules That Matter Most

You do not have to hand out an IABS just for someone browsing your open house. Confirmed directly by TREC — a license holder is not required to provide the statutory Information About Brokerage Services form merely for an open house visit.TRELA §1101.558(c)(3)
But a written buyer agreement is required before you actually show property to a buyer. Since January 1, 2026, this applies whether or not you end up representing them — the agreement can be a full representation agreement, or a 14-day, non-exclusive, non-representation "showing only" agreement.Occ. Code §1101.563

Where open houses fit in: An open house is the practical, recognized setting where non-representation showing-only agreements get used — a buyer can walk through without you representing them, so long as you stick to facts (square footage, price, listed terms) and don't cross into opinions or advice. The moment a buyer asks "what do you think of this kitchen?" and you answer with anything beyond what's on the MLS, you've provided a brokerage service — and now need a full written agreement, not just the showing-only kind.

Who's Actually Holding the Open House

This changes what compliance risk exists, and for which broker.

You hold your own listing open

Standard rules apply exactly as they would for any other showing. If an unrepresented buyer walks in and you stick to facts, no written agreement is needed yet. The moment you give an opinion or advice, or agree (orally or in writing) to represent them, you've triggered the §1101.563 written agreement requirement — and since you already represent the seller on this property, representing this buyer too makes your broker an intermediary in this transaction.

A same-broker teammate holds it (not the listing agent)

This is the classic setup for intermediary with appointments. If an unrepresented buyer at the open house ends up wanting to be represented, and your broker also represents the seller through you as listing agent, your broker can appoint that teammate to the buyer and appoint you to the seller — two different license holders under the same broker, each free to give advice and opinions to their own appointed party.§1101.560-561

Before that happens, all of these must occur: IABS presented to both parties at first substantive contact; the seller's listing agreement authorizes the broker to act as intermediary and states the prohibited conduct in bold or underlined print; the buyer's written agreement does the same.§1101.651(d) Without appointments, neither agent may offer advice or opinions to either party, and must not favor one side over the other.

An agent from a different brokerage holds it (covering as a favor)

Since subagency was eliminated statewide in 2026, this covering agent is not automatically anyone's agent just by sitting the open house — they don't represent your seller, and no intermediary issue can arise for your broker, since that agent isn't sponsored by your broker at all.

Two things worth having a real policy on: whether your brokerage even allows agents to cover open houses for other firms (TREC has confirmed this is entirely up to each broker's own policy), and what that covering agent is authorized to say about the listing if a buyer asks. Absent an explicit cooperation agreement, that covering agent has no authority to bind or represent your seller — if a buyer at that open house wants representation, it would typically be through the covering agent's own broker, as an ordinary cooperating-broker relationship from there.

Appointment vs. Non-Appointment

The difference is simple but consequential: appointments let each agent actually advise their own side.

Appointments madeWhat each agent can do
With appointments Each appointed license holder may give advice and opinions to the party they've been appointed to — a real, working representation on each side, even though one broker sits over both.
Without appointments Neither the broker nor any sponsored agent may give advice or opinions to either party on the transaction, and must not favor one principal over the other. The broker simply facilitates.

One rule that trips people up: a broker can't appoint themselves to represent one side while appointing an associate to the other — that's not considered fair and impartial under the statute. Appointments have to go to two different associated license holders, never the broker paired with an associate.

Timing matters too: appointments should be made before the buyer starts receiving advice from their appointed agent — not after the fact.

What About "Teams"?

A team name is a marketing construct — it doesn't change any of the agency or intermediary rules above.

A team is not a separate brokerage. Every agent on a team is still individually sponsored by the same one broker. If two teammates end up on opposite sides of the same deal — one representing the buyer, one representing the seller — that is exactly the same-broker-teammate intermediary scenario described above, with the exact same requirements: IABS to both parties, a listing agreement and buyer agreement that authorize the broker to act as intermediary, and proper appointments before either teammate gives advice.Rule 535.154

Team branding can make this easy to lose track of — "the Smith Team helped us buy and sell" can sound like two different companies when it's actually one broker on both sides of the table. The intermediary paperwork still has to happen; the team name doesn't substitute for it.

Team name requirements Must end in "Team" or "Group"; can't contain words like "Realty," "Brokerage," "Company," or "Associates" that imply an independent brokerage; must be registered with TREC by the sponsoring broker before it's used in any advertisement.Rule 535.154(c)
Advertising disclosure Every ad must name the license holder or team placing it, and the sponsoring broker's name must appear at least half the size of the largest team or agent contact information in that ad.Rule 535.155

Can the Listing Agent Switch to Represent the Buyer?

Mechanically possible with the right paperwork — but this is where the confidentiality problem gets serious.

The setup: the agent who took the listing later shows the property to a buyer, then switches to represent that buyer — while a different sponsored agent under the same broker steps in to represent the seller in their place.

Structurally, this is the same intermediary-with-appointments framework already covered above, just with the roles reassigned: the broker appoints the newly-brought-in agent to the seller, and appoints the original listing agent to the buyer, with all the same written consent and disclosure steps required either way.

The real problem: confidentiality doesn't reset when representation does Confidential information the original agent received while representing the seller — their motivation for selling, their real bottom-line price, negotiating position — cannot be disclosed to the new client, the buyer, even after the switch. The agent doesn't stop knowing it just because their role changed.

This is exactly the instinct worth trusting: even with every consent form signed correctly, that same individual is now expected to advocate for the buyer against a seller who trusted them with confidential information days or weeks earlier. That's difficult to police in practice and creates a serious appearance of conflict even when handled by the book.

Many brokerages address this with a firm-wide policy rather than deciding it case by case — for example, prohibiting the original listing agent from ever becoming the buyer's agent on their own listing, regardless of who ends up representing the seller instead. Worth having an explicit position on this in your own Policies & Procedures rather than leaving it to individual judgment calls.

What Is a Management Certificate?

A management certificate is a document recorded with the county clerk that provides key information about how an HOA (or "property owners' association," POA) is managed — things like the association's name and mailing address, the name and contact information of whoever manages it, and where to find the association's governing documents.

Who has to filePOAs governed by Chapter 209 of the Texas Property CodeProp. Code §209.004
Condo associationsGoverned separately under Chapter 82, with their own required contentsProp. Code §82.116
Filing deadlineMust file with TREC no later than 7 days after recording with the county clerk
Who regulates HOAsNot TREC — TREC only maintains the database, with no licensing or enforcement authority over HOAs

Why This Exists

The 87th Texas Legislature passed SB 1588, amending Chapter 209 of the Property Code to require HOAs to record a management certificate with their county clerk, and then also electronically file that certificate with TREC — making the information searchable in one place instead of scattered across county records.

What TREC Does — and Doesn't — Do

TREC maintains the database. hoa.texas.gov is where HOAs upload their certificates, and where the public can search by location or association name.

TREC does not regulate HOAs. TREC has no licensing or enforcement authority over HOA activities — if a certificate is missing, wrong, or an HOA is behaving badly, that's not something TREC can act on.

What's Required in a Certificate

Property Code Section 209.004 lists what a POA's management certificate must include§209.004 — commonly cited items are:

  • The name and mailing address of the association
  • The name and contact information (phone and email) of the person or company managing the association, or an officer if self-managed
  • The website address where the association's governing documents (declaration, bylaws, rules) can be accessed
  • Fee information related to the transfer of property within the association

Condo unit owners' associations have a similar but separately-defined list under Section 82.116§82.116 for associations governed by Chapter 82 rather than Chapter 209.

If a Certificate Isn't Available

Not every HOA has filed yet, and some are exempt or still working through the process. If a search on hoa.texas.gov doesn't turn up a certificate for a property's association, that doesn't necessarily mean the HOA doesn't exist or isn't required to file — it may not have completed its filing. Chapter 209 outlines consequences for associations that fail to file as requiredCh. 209, but tracking that down for a specific HOA is outside what this page or TREC's database can resolve — that's a research or legal question, not a database gap you can just wait out.

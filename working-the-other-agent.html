Working the Other Agent

Email Templates for the Offer & Option Period

The deal usually isn't won or lost on price — it's won or lost on whether the other agent wants to work with you. These templates are built to answer their real question ("is this going to be easy or a headache?") before they even ask it, especially during the first 7–10 days before the option period expires, when tone matters most.

1. Initial Offer Submission

Goal: Get the listing agent to walk into their seller conversation already on your side.

Why this works: Leads with credibility (not price), makes their job easy with a clean summary, and closes with genuine availability rather than pressure.
Email Template
Subject: Offer on [Property Address] — [Buyer Last Name] Hi [Listing Agent Name], Attached is our offer on [Property Address]. A quick summary before you dig into the contract: • Offer price: $[amount] • Earnest money: $[amount] • Option fee: $[amount] / [#] day option period • Financing: [Pre-approved conventional / Cash / etc.] — [Lender Name] letter attached • Closing date: [date] • [Any other standout term — flexible move-in, as-is, etc.] A little about my buyer: [1-2 sentences — genuinely humanizing, not just "motivated buyer."] I know you'll have questions or maybe a counter — I'm easy to reach all week, happy to hop on a call if that's faster than emailing back and forth. Really hope this one works out for everyone. Thanks for taking a look, [Your Name] [Phone] | [Email]

In a competitive situation, add near the top: "I know you're likely getting more than one offer on this one — happy to make this easy on your end, just let me know what would help." That single line signals you won't fight them on process.

2. The Quiet-Listing-Agent Check-In

Goal: Nudge for an update without sounding anxious or pushy.

Why this works: No urgency language, gives them an easy out, and offers to help rather than asking "what's taking so long."
Email Template
Subject: Checking In — [Property Address] Hi [Listing Agent Name], Just checking in on where things stand with [buyer name]'s offer on [Property Address] — totally understand if your sellers are still weighing things or waiting on other offers, no rush at all on my end. Wanted to make sure the offer came through okay, and see if there's anything else — a different term, more info on my buyer, anything — that would help you present it. Whenever you have an update, I'm around. Thanks! [Your Name] [Phone] | [Email]

One check-in is a nudge. Two in a row, without a response, starts to read as pressure — if there's still no answer after this, a phone call is usually a better move than a third email.

3. Option Period Repair/Amendment Request

Goal: Frame inspection findings as information-sharing, not an attack on the house.

Why this works: Leads with what's fine about the house, attributes findings to the inspector (not your buyer's opinion), and offers the request as a starting point rather than a demand.
Email Template
Subject: [Property Address] — Inspection Follow-Up Hi [Listing Agent Name], Inspection went well overall — the house is in solid shape and my buyers are still very much moving forward. A few items came up that the inspector flagged as worth addressing before closing; amendment attached. The big ones: • [Item 1 — brief, factual, no editorializing] • [Item 2] • [Item 3 if needed — keep this to the real issues, not every minor note] Full inspection report is attached if your sellers want to see the details firsthand rather than just my summary. We're not trying to renegotiate the whole deal here — just want to get these squared away so we can get to closing smoothly. Open to a credit instead of repairs if that's easier on your sellers' end, whatever works best for everyone. Let me know how your sellers want to handle it, happy to talk through any of it. Thanks, [Your Name] [Phone] | [Email]

If the seller pushes back hard, resist escalating over email. A quick agent-to-agent call almost always resolves a stuck repair negotiation faster, and lets you read whether they're actually firm or just pushing back once as part of the process.

Working the Gatekeeper Personality

  • Lead with the human context, not just the numbers. A price and a story lands better than a price alone — gatekeeper agents are pattern-matching for "easy buyer" vs. "problem buyer."
  • Never make the listing agent choose between you and their seller. Frame requests as things the seller can decide, not things you're insisting on.
  • Match their pace, don't set it. Repeated follow-ups read as pressure and can work against you.
  • Once you're through the option period, the tone can relax. Both sides are now committed — save the extra diplomatic effort for these first 7–10 days when it matters most.

Smart Home Devices & Recording During Showings

Ring, Nest, and similar devices are in most homes now — here's what the law actually says, and what to check before every showing.

⚠️ Not Legal Advice
This page is a practice aid only and is not intended as, and should not be relied on as, legal advice. Unlawful interception under Tex. Penal Code §16.02 is a felony (second-degree generally; state jail felony for certain subsections) — 2 to 20 years and a fine up to $10,000. Separately, Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code §123.004 entitles a victim to sue for statutory damages of $10,000 per occurrence as a floor, not a cap — plus actual damages above that, punitive damages, and attorney's fees and court costs. If a specific situation has already happened, or you're unsure how this applies to a real transaction, contact your broker or a licensed attorney before taking any action — do not rely on this guide alone.
Owning the Device Doesn't Make You a Party to the Conversation
Why This Matters
Texas is "one-party consent"

Recording is legal if you're actually part of the conversation, or someone in it consented. It has nothing to do with who owns the recording device.

A seller's own camera doesn't get a pass

A Ring doorbell capturing a buyer's private conversation with their own agent isn't legal just because the seller owns the hardware — neither the buyer nor their agent consented, and the seller isn't part of that conversation at all.

Video-only is a different, legal category

Plain video surveillance of your own property (no audio) is well-established and legal — the same as any homeowner's security camera. Audio is what changes the legal picture, because private conversation carries an expectation of privacy that being seen doesn't.

This Protects You, Not Just the Buyer
Why Accurate MLS Disclosure Matters to the Seller
Accurate disclosure can support implied consent

If the MLS field correctly discloses audio recording and that reaches the buyer through their agent, a buyer who proceeds anyway has a real argument for implied consent — which can shift recording from illegal interception to legal.

A blank or inaccurate field next to an active device is worse than having no device at all

It reads less like an oversight and more like concealment — a materially different, weaker legal position than simply disclosing and moving forward.

Disclosure is a backstop, not a substitute for disabling audio

It only works if the disclosure genuinely reaches the buyer — not just exists in an MLS field their agent glanced past. Courts haven't clearly settled that a buried field alone is adequate notice. Disable audio during showings regardless of what's disclosed.

The listing agent's real job here is accuracy, not just asking once

Confirm the MLS field matches reality at listing time — and again if the seller adds a device later in the listing period.

Before Every Listing Goes Live
What to Ask Every Seller
Ask directly, don't assume

Ring, Nest, Google Home/Alexa devices, and video doorbells are common enough now that this should be a standard listing-prep question, not a special case.

Ask about every room, not just the front door

Smart speakers and cameras show up in living rooms, kitchens, and home offices — not just entryways.

If the seller adds a new device later

Confirm the field again — a listing that was accurate at intake can become inaccurate mid-listing if a seller adds a doorbell camera or wired system after going live.

Wired installs require a licensed installer

If a seller hires a professional to install a wired surveillance system, Texas law requires that installer to hold a license through the Texas DPS Private Security Bureau. Worth knowing before casually recommending a handyman for the job.

Professional installs are usually already safe — DIY is where the real risk sits

Most professional installers already know this law, avoid audio recording, and are hesitant to place cameras anywhere but a foyer. The real question worth asking a seller is whether they set up the system themselves — that's where an accidental audio-recording problem is most likely to actually exist.

Sample Script

"Before we start showing the home, I need to ask — do you have any Ring, Nest, smart speakers, or other devices that could record audio anywhere inside? We'll need those switched to video-only or turned off before any showing, so buyers can speak freely with their own agent."

Disable Audio, Not Necessarily Video
The Fix
Most devices let you turn off audio while keeping video

This preserves the legitimate security benefit (who's coming and going) without capturing conversations.

If audio can't be disabled separately, turn the device off entirely during the showing window

Better to lose video coverage for a few hours than leave an active microphone running.

Never run video and audio together during a showing

Even where silent video alone would be fine, combining it with audio recording during an active showing is exactly the setup that turns a legal security camera into an illegal recording — per Texas REALTORS®' own guidance to sellers.

Ring

Settings → Video Settings → toggle off "Record Audio" per device, or "Do Not Disturb" mode during showings.

Google Nest

Settings → Home info → Audio toggle off per camera in the Google Home app.

Amazon Alexa / Echo

Mute button (physical, on the device) disables the microphone entirely — the simplest option.

Video doorbells (generic)

Check for an audio-specific toggle before assuming "off" means the whole device is off.

Protecting Your Own Client
For Buyer's Agents Too
Ask before entering any occupied showing

This is now standard practice industry-wide, not overcautious — smart devices are common enough that assuming a home is "unmiked" is no longer safe.

If in doubt, save candid conversation for outside

Discuss real reactions, strategy, and offer thinking once you're back in the car — not inside a home that might still be listening.
